  1. Reagents/Buffers/Materials Needed for Experiments

    1. A complete electrically quiet electrophysiological setup
      1. A vibration isolate table
      2. An inverted (or an upright) microscope
      3. An inline-perfusion solution heater
      4. A microscope stage heater
      5. A low-speed peristaltic pump
      6. A vacuum source (a house vacuum line or a vacuum pump) and a waste flask
      7. A micromanipulator
      8. A patch-clamp amplifier with its head stage assembly mounted on the aforementioned micromanipulator
      9. An AD/DA converter (if not integrated into the patch-clamp amplifier)
      10. A data acquisition program
      11. A recording chamber with a Ag/AgCl pellet ground wire
    2. Pipette puller (Sutter-P97 or equivalent)
    3. Electrode tip polisher (e.g. Narashige)
    4. Sylgard 184 elastomer or dental was to coat electrodes to reduce capacitance
    5. Patch-clamp glass (e.g., Warner Instruments patch glass)
    6. A small bath sonicator
    7. A dissection microscope
    8. A diamond pen and a pair of tweezers
    9. Recording solutions
      1. No glucose external solution
        2. 130 mM NaCl, 4 mM KCl, 2 CaCl2, 2 MgCl2, 30 mM mannitol, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.4 with N-methyl-D-glucamine at 35°C

      2. 5 mM glucose external solution
        3. 130 mM NaCl, 4 mM KCl, 2 CaCl2, 2 MgCl2, 15 mM mannitol, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.4 with N-methyl-D-glucamine at 35°C

      3. 25 mM glucose external solution
        4. 130 mM NaCl, 4 mM KCl, 2 CaCl2, 2 MgCl2, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.4 with N-methyl-D-glucamine at 35°C

      4. Sulfate internal solution
        5. 76 K2SO4 mM, 10 mM KCl, 10 mM NaCl, 6 MgCl2, 30 mM mannitol, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.4 with N-methyl-D-glucamine at 35°C

        Note:
        Based on the Ca2+ chelating abilities of sulfate, the free Ca2+ concentration is estimated to be in the low µM range

    10. β-escin (8 mM in water)
      11. The final concentration is 8 µM; diluted with the internal solution before each recording session and sonicate

  2. Procedure

    1. Sonicate 8 μM escin diluted with the internal solution in the bath sonicator for >10 min
      2. 4 µM escin is sometimes sufficient
    2. Start the perfusion of the recording chamber so that the chamber solution is at 35°C; the perfusion is continuous
    3. Take a culture dish with a coverslip with pancreatic islets (or pancreatic islet cells) using the dissection microscope
    4. Cut out a small section, with an islet (or cells), using a diamond pen, and transfer the coverslip piece to the recording chamber filled with the desired recording solution (e.g., 5 mM glucose)
    5. Equilibrate the islet (or cells) in the chamber for >10 min
    6. Fill the tip of a polished, sylgard- or wax-coated patch electrode with the internal recording without escin and back fill with the recording with escin
    7. The input resistance of the electrode should be 3 to 6 Mohms, depending on the types of the data required; lower for voltage-clamp experiments and higher for current-clamp experiments
    8. Form a seal with a few Gohms in resistance
    9. Change the holding voltage to–70 mV (in the whole-cell mode convention) and apply small short square pulses (–10 mV in size from the holding voltage and 20 ms in duration) to monitor the resistance and capacitance
    10. Within 5 to 10 min, adequate whole-cell access should be achieved
    11. Apply short small square pulses in the voltage-clamp mode ( ­–10mV in size from the holding voltage and 20ms in duration) to measure the input resistance and capacitance
    12. Compensate the whole-cell capacitance and series resistance
    13. Measure Vm in the current-clamp mode; change the external solutions if desired
    14. Measure Im in the voltage-clamp mode; change the external solutions if desired
